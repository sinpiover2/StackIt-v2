Stack It!

Remember to think about The Learning Space!

Play the game!

  • Choose the number of discs (3–8) using the arrows.
  • Drag only the top disc from any stack.
  • You can’t place a larger disc on a smaller one.
  • Goal: move the whole stack onto one target pole (middle or right).
  • Click Restart to try a new disc count or improve your strategy.
Challenges
  • Can you solve it with 3? 4? 5? … 8 discs?
  • Can you do it efficiently? Track your moves and try to improve.
  • How does the difficulty change as discs increase?

Math prompts & investigations

  • What are the rules? Restate them in your own words.
  • What can/can’t you do? Give examples of legal and illegal moves.
  • Predict then test: Before you start with n discs, estimate the number of moves you’ll need. Were you close?
  • Notice & Wonder: What patterns do you notice as n grows? What do you wonder?
  • Look for structure: How is the n-disc puzzle built from the (n−1)-disc puzzle?
  • Conjecture a rule: Find a general relationship between n and a “best possible” move count. Justify your conjecture.
  • Efficiency: Describe a strategy that always succeeds. Why does it avoid illegal moves?
  • Representation: Draw a diagram or write pseudocode for your method.
  • Compare strategies: Are there different strategies that result in the same minimal moves? Explain.
  • Extension: If either the middle or right pole is allowed as a target, does the minimum change? Why or why not?
  • Generalize: How many moves would it take for 9, 10, or 20 discs? Explain your reasoning without playing.
Teacher notes (hidden)
  • Minimum moves (classic target on one pole): \(2^{\,n}-1\). Invite students to notice doubling +1 across rows (3→7→15…). Connect to recursive structure: solve (n−1), move largest, solve (n−1).
  • Middle or right as target: Minimum remains \(2^{\,n}-1\) (choice of target doesn’t change optimal count).
  • Talk moves: “Say more…”, “What do you notice that makes you say that?”, “Can someone restate their idea?”
  • Productive struggle: Encourage students to start with n=3, then 4, then predict 5 before playing.
  • Evidence: Ask for a diagram or step pattern for 3→4→5 showing the recursive “build from smaller” idea.
  • Extensions: binary counting connection; invariant reasoning; write & justify a recurrence \(T(n)=2T(n-1)+1\); optional proof by induction.
